Projectile Motion

Introduction

Projectile motion is a type of two dimensional motion. As a projectile moves horizontally, it also moves vertically as it rises or falls through the air. Most introductory physics problems will neglect the effect of air resistance.

It is important to note that motion in the horizontal direction is independent of motion in the vertical direction. MythBusters has a great episode where they test this notion by dropping a bullet and firing a bullet from the same height:

Two Types of Projectile Motion

The two types of projectile motion that you will encounter are elevated horizontal launches and angled launches. The figure below shows the two types of projectile motion launched with the same horizontal velocity (vx).

What are some similarities and differences between these two curves?
  1. Both projectiles travel the same horizontal distance. This is because they have the same horizontal velocity!
  2. Both projectiles have some maximum height where the vertical velocity is zero.
  3. The elevated horizontal launch has no initial vertical velocity.

Types of Projectile Motion Problems

At most, there's only four types of projectile motion problems:

  • How Far?
  • How High?
  • How Much Time?
  • How Fast?

"How Far?" (horizontal displacement)

To find out how far a projectile travels horizontally, you need to know two things: time (t) and horizontal velocity (vx). In these types of problems, the initial velocity (vi ) is the same as the horizontal velocity (vx ). If you are lucky, then you will be given both time and horizontal velocity in the problem, but don't count on it! It is possible that you may have to calculate time first before being able to solve for the horiontal displacement. To find out how to calculate time, jump down to "How Much Time?" below.

The equation that allows us to calculate how far a projectile moves in the horizontal direction is:

\(\texttip{\Delta x}{displacement (m)} = \texttip{v_x}{horizontal velocity (m/s)} \texttip{t}{time (s)}\)

Some sources may choose to use d (for distance) instead of Δx (for displacement):

\(\texttip{d}{distance (m)} = \texttip{v_x}{horizontal velocity (m/s)} \texttip{t}{time (s)}\)

The Range Equation

For angled launches, you also have the option using something known as the range equation. This equation is designed to work for angled launches where the projectile lands at the same elevation as it was launched. Notice that the initial angle is doubled in this equation. So for an angle of 45 degrees, you will be taking the sin of 90 degrees. A 45 degree angle by the way, gives a maximum value of the range.

\(\texttip{\Delta x}{displacement (m)} = \texttip{\frac{v_{i}^2}{g}}{square of the initial velocity (m/s) divided by gravity(9.8 m/s^2)} \texttip{sin2\Theta}{sine of twice the initial angle}\)

Scroll over the equations above if you are unfamiliar with what the symbols represent.

Example 1: A ball rolls off the edge with an
initial velocity of 2 m/s. initial velocity = horizontal velocity for horizontal launches!
If the ball takes
1 second time
to fall to the ground, how far from the edge of the table did the ball land?

What information does the problem give us? Solve the problem.

vx = 2 m/s, and t = 1 s

From this information, we can solve for displacement, which is Δx = vxt = 2 * 1 = 2 meters


Example 2: A ball is shot into the air at a
53 degree angle with an initial velocity of 5 m/s. knowing the angle and the initial velocity, we can use the range equation!
If the ball lands at the same elevation as it was launched, how far did the ball travel?

What information does the problem give us, and which equation is the best to use?

The problem gives us the initial velocity and the initial angle. Along with the fact that the problem tells us that the ball lands at the same elevation makes this a good candidate for using the range equation.

\(\Delta x = \frac{v_{i}^2}{g} = \Delta x = \frac{(5 m/s)^2}{9.8 m/s^2\)
